Subtopic 8.2: Types Of Threats

8.2.1 - Understand potential technical threats and their impacts on organisations and individuals including prevention and mitigation methods:

Technical threats refer to various cyber threats that can compromise the security, integrity, and availability of systems and data.
Common technical threats include:

  • Malware: Malicious software that can damage or disrupt systems.
      Examples: viruses, worms, key loggers, ransomware, spyware, trojans.
  • DoS Attacks: Denial of Service attacks are when one computer overwhelms a system with traffic, making it unavailable to users.
      (Distributed Denial of Service [DDoS] attacks are DoS attacks that come from several computers)
  • BotNets: Networks of compromised computers controlled by attackers to launch large-scale attacks or spread malware.
      Example Usages: Used for DDoS attacks, spamming, and distributing malware.
  • Hackers: Individuals or groups who attempt to gain unauthorized access to systems or data.
      Example Usages: Can exploit vulnerabilities to steal, alter, or destroy information.
  • Social Engineering: Manipulating people into revealing confidential information or performing actions that compromise security.
      Examples: phishing, pretexting, baiting, tailgating.
  • Phishing: Fraudulent attempts to obtain sensitive information by pretending to be a trustworthy entity, often via email.
      Example Usages: Can lead to identity theft, financial loss, and unauthorized access to systems.
  • Insecure Application Programming Interfaces (APIs): APIs that lack proper security controls can be exploited to access or manipulate data.
  • Man-In-The-Middle attacks: Attackers intercept and potentially alter communications between two parties without their knowledge.
  • Open/Unsecured Wi-Fi networks: Public or poorly secured wireless networks can be exploited by attackers to intercept data or gain unauthorized access.

8.2.2 - Understand potential technical vulnerabilities to systems and data:

Technical vulnerabilities are weaknesses in systems, software, or processes that can be exploited by attackers to compromise security.
Common technical vulnerabilities include:

  • Inadequate Security Processes: Poorly implemented or missing security procedures can leave systems exposed.
      Examples: Lack of regular security audits, insufficient monitoring, failure to update security policies.
  • Weak Encryption: Using outdated or easily broken encryption algorithms makes data susceptible to interception and theft.
      Example Usages: Transmitting sensitive information over unsecured channels, storing passwords with weak hashes.
  • Inadequate Password Policy: Allowing weak, easily guessable passwords or not enforcing password changes increases risk of unauthorized access.
      Examples: No minimum password length, no complexity requirements, passwords reused across accounts.
  • Failure to Use Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA): Relying solely on passwords makes accounts more vulnerable to compromise.
      Example Usages: Sensitive accounts accessible with only a password, no additional verification steps.
  • Out-of-Date Components: Using outdated hardware, software, or firmware can introduce vulnerabilities due to lack of support or unpatched security flaws.
      Examples: Unsupported operating systems, legacy applications, unpatched zero-day bugs, obsolete firmware.

8.2.3 - Understand potential human threats, including prevention and mitigation methods, to systems and data:

Human threats refer to risks posed by individuals, either through mistakes or intentional actions, that can compromise systems and data.
Common human threats include:

  • Human Error: Mistakes made by users or staff can lead to data loss, breaches, or system failures.
      Examples: Accidentally deleting files, misconfiguring system settings, sending sensitive information to the wrong recipient.
      Prevention & Mitigation: Use file properties to restrict access, implement confirmation boxes for critical actions, provide regular staff training.
  • Malicious Employee: Staff members who intentionally harm systems or steal data.
      Examples: Sabotaging systems, stealing confidential information, installing malware.
      Prevention & Mitigation: Immediate removal from premises, suspend user accounts immediately, monitor user activity.
  • Disguised Criminal: Individuals who gain physical access to premises by pretending to be legitimate visitors.
      Examples: Impersonating staff, contractors, or delivery personnel to access restricted areas.
      Prevention & Mitigation: Accompany all visitors, check identification of visitors, enforce visitor sign-in procedures.
  • Poor Cyber Hygiene: Unsafe habits and practices that increase vulnerability to threats.
      Examples: Leaving machines unlocked, writing down passwords, using weak or reused passwords.
      Prevention & Mitigation: Lock all unattended machines, avoid writing down passwords, enforce strong password management policies.

8.2.4 - Understand potential physical vulnerabilities, including prevention and mitigation methods, to systems, data and information, including:

Physical vulnerabilities refer to weaknesses in the physical environment or controls that can be exploited to compromise systems, data, or information.
Common physical vulnerabilities include:

  • Lack of Access Control: Inadequate systems for controlling entry to secure areas can allow unauthorized individuals to gain access.
      Prevention & Mitigation: Implement entry control systems such as key cards, biometric scanners, or security personnel.
  • Poor Access Control: Weak or poorly enforced access procedures increase the risk of unauthorized access.
      Prevention & Mitigation:
    • Do not allow tailgating (following someone through a secure door without authorization).
    • Use complex access codes and change them regularly.
    • Monitor access areas with CCTV or security staff.
    • Conduct regular audits of staff access to secure areas.
  • Nature of Location: The physical environment can introduce risks such as shoulder surfing, environmental hazards, or vandalism.
      Prevention & Mitigation:
    • Protect against shoulder surfing by using privacy screens and positioning devices securely.
    • Safeguard equipment and data from environmental threats (e.g., water, fire, dust).
    • Implement measures to deter and prevent vandalism, such as secure enclosures and surveillance.
  • Poor System Robustness: Using equipment that is not designed to withstand harsh conditions can lead to failures or data loss.
      Prevention & Mitigation: Use rugged machines and hardware designed for durability in challenging environments.
  • Natural Disasters: Events such as floods, fires, or earthquakes can damage systems and data.
      Prevention & Mitigation: Develop disaster recovery plans, use backup systems, and store critical data offsite or in the cloud.

8.2.5 - Understand the potential impact to an organisation of threats and vulnerabilities:

The potential impact to an organisation of threats and vulnerabilities can be significant, affecting operations, reputation, and finances.
Common impacts include:

  • Loss/Leaking of Sensitive Data: Confidential information may be exposed or stolen, leading to legal consequences, reputational damage, and loss of customer trust.
      Examples: Personal data breaches, intellectual property theft, exposure of financial records.
  • Unauthorised Access to Digital Systems: Attackers may gain access to systems, allowing them to manipulate, steal, or destroy data.
      Examples: Hackers accessing databases, altering system configurations, installing malware.
  • Data Corruption: Data may be intentionally or accidentally altered, making it unusable or unreliable.
      Examples: Malware corrupting files, accidental overwriting of records, sabotage by malicious insiders.
  • Disruption of Service: Threats can cause systems or services to become unavailable, impacting productivity and customer experience.
      Examples: Denial of Service (DoS) attacks, hardware failures, ransomware locking systems.
  • Unauthorised Access to Restricted Physical Areas: Physical security breaches can result in theft, sabotage, or further cyber attacks.
      Examples: Intruders accessing server rooms, stealing devices, planting malicious hardware.
