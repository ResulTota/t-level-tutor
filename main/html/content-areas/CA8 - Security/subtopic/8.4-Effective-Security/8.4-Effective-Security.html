Subtopic 8.4: Interrelationship of Components Required for Effective Security

8.4.1 - Understand how the relationships in the CIA triad interrelate.

The CIA triad—Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability—forms the foundation of effective information security.
These three components are deeply interconnected:

  • Protecting confidentiality helps maintain data integrity.
  • Ensuring integrity supports availability.
A weakness in one area can undermine the others, so a balanced approach is essential.
Understanding how these elements work together enables organisations to safeguard sensitive information, maintain trustworthiness, and ensure reliable access to data and systems.
  • Confidentiality:
    Ensuring that data is kept private by controlling who has access to the data.
      Techniques: Encryption, access controls, authentication, data classification.
      Benefits: Protects sensitive information from unauthorized disclosure.
      Drawbacks: May introduce complexity and reduce usability if not managed properly.

  • Integrity:
    Ensuring that the data has not been tampered with; this can be done by maintaining confidentiality.
      Techniques: Hashing, digital signatures, checksums, audit trails.
      Benefits: Maintains trustworthiness and accuracy of data.
      Drawbacks: Can require additional resources and may impact performance.

  • Availability:
    Ensuring that data is available and useful; this can be done by ensuring integrity.
      Techniques: Redundancy, backups, failover systems, disaster recovery planning.
      Benefits: Ensures users can access data and services when needed.
      Drawbacks: May increase costs and require ongoing maintenance.
Relationship:
The CIA triad components support each other; for example, maintaining confidentiality helps protect integrity, and ensuring integrity supports availability. Weakness in one area can undermine the others.

8.4.2 - Understand the processes and procedures that assure internet security and the reasons why they are used.

Understand the elements of the Identification, Authentication, Authorisation, Accountability (IAAA) model, including the techniques used, benefits, and drawbacks:

  • Identification:
    Recognising the individual within a digital system.
      Techniques:
    • Knowledge-based identification (e.g., username)
    • Possession-based identification (e.g., ID cards, tokens)
    • Biometric-based identification (e.g., fingerprint, facial recognition)
      Benefits: Enables systems to distinguish between users and apply appropriate controls.
      Drawbacks: May be vulnerable to impersonation or theft of credentials/devices.

  • Authentication:
    Verifying the identity claimed during the identification phase.
      Techniques:
    • Passwords and passphrases
    • Multi-factor authentication (MFA)
    • Biometric authentication
      Benefits: Strengthens access control by requiring proof of identity.
      Drawbacks: Passwords can be weak or reused; biometrics may raise privacy concerns.

  • Authorisation:
    Ensuring that authenticated users can only access resources and perform actions they are permitted to.
      Techniques:
    • Role-based access control (RBAC)
    • Access control lists (ACLs)
      Benefits: Limits exposure of sensitive data and functions to only those who need them.
      Drawbacks: Can be complex to manage and maintain, especially in large organisations.

  • Accountability:
    Ensuring that any actions within a system can be traced back to the responsible user.
      Techniques:
    • Audit logs
    • User activity monitoring
      Benefits: Deters misuse and supports investigations by providing traceability.
      Drawbacks: Requires secure storage and management of logs; privacy concerns may arise.
Relationship:
The IAAA components work together to ensure secure access and accountability in digital systems. Weakness in one area can compromise the effectiveness of the others.
