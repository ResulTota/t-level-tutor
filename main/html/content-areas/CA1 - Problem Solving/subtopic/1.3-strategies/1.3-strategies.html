1.3.1 - Know the different approaches to solving problems and understand their purpose and when they are used

1.3.2 - Know the benefits and drawbacks of using the different approaches to solving problems

Top-Down Approach

Definition: The problem is broken down into smaller, more manageable sub-problems, working from the overall design down to the details.

Purpose: Ensures that the main structure and logic of the solution are considered before focusing on specific details.

Benefits:

  • Provides a clear structure for the solution.
  • Helps avoid missing steps in the design.
  • Makes large problems easier to manage by breaking them into smaller parts.

Drawbacks:

  • Detailed issues may only be discovered late in the process.
  • Revisions may be needed if initial high-level design is flawed.
  • Can be time-consuming if constant restructuring is required.

Bottom-Up Approach

Definition: Focuses on building individual modules or components first and then combining them to form the overall solution.

Purpose: Useful when reliable modules already exist and can be reused to speed up development.

Benefits:

  • Encourages code reuse of existing components.
  • Can speed up development if modules are pre-built.
  • Testing can begin earlier at the component level.

Drawbacks:

  • Risk of poor system integration if design is not planned properly.
  • May result in duplicated effort if modules overlap in functionality.
  • Lack of big-picture planning can lead to inefficiency.

Modularisation

Definition: Divides a problem into independent, reusable modules, each performing a specific function.

Purpose: Helps simplify complex problems by allowing developers to focus on smaller parts that can be reused across different projects.

Benefits:

  • Improves maintainability and readability of code.
  • Facilitates debugging and testing by isolating functionality.
  • Supports team collaboration by allowing multiple people to work on separate modules.
  • Encourages code reuse across different programs.

Drawbacks:

  • Requires careful planning to define module boundaries.
  • Managing too many modules can add complexity.
  • Can introduce integration overhead and dependencies.

1.3.3 - Understand the purpose of root cause analysis and when it is used

1.3.4 - Know and understand approaches to root cause analysis

Root Cause Analysis (RCA): A structured process used to identify the underlying cause of a problem, rather than just addressing its symptoms.
Purpose: Helps prevent issues from recurring, improves processes, and is used after failures, incidents, or recurring errors.

Five Whys

Definition: A questioning technique where “Why?” is asked repeatedly (typically five times) until the root cause of a problem is found.

Purpose: To simplify investigation and quickly drill down to the true source of an issue.

Benefits:

  • Simple method requiring no tools.
  • Encourages critical thinking.
  • Quick to apply in meetings or discussions.

Drawbacks:

  • May lead to superficial answers if not applied carefully.
  • Subjective - depends on the knowledge of the people involved.
  • Not always effective for complex problems.

Failure Mode and Effects Analysis (FMEA)

Definition: A structured technique that identifies possible failures in a system, their causes, and their consequences, then prioritises them by risk level.

Purpose: To proactively prevent failures by addressing high-risk areas before they occur.

Benefits:

  • Provides a systematic approach to problem prevention.
  • Helps prioritise risks based on severity and likelihood.
  • Improves product reliability and safety.

Drawbacks:

  • Can be time-consuming and resource heavy.
  • Requires detailed knowledge of the system.
  • Risk of over-analysis on less critical issues.

Event Tree Analysis (ETA)

Definition: A forward-looking analysis that starts with an initiating event and maps out all possible outcomes and consequences using a tree diagram.

Purpose: To anticipate and evaluate potential consequences of failures, helping organisations plan responses and mitigations.

Benefits:

  • Provides a visual structure of possible outcomes.
  • Useful for risk assessment and contingency planning.
  • Can handle complex systems with multiple outcomes.

Drawbacks:

  • Can be complex to create and interpret.
  • Relies on accurate data to be effective.
  • May become large diagrams that are difficult to manage.

Actions After Root Cause Analysis

Once the RCA is completed, organisations must take appropriate actions to ensure problems are addressed and prevented in the future:

  • Log: Record the findings and decisions for accountability and future reference.
  • Close: Confirm that corrective actions have been taken and the issue is resolved.
  • Escalate: Pass unresolved or serious issues to the appropriate manager, specialist, or external third party.

1.3.5 - Know and understand the process of the high-level problem-solving strategy

1. Define the Problem

Clearly identify what the problem is. Ask questions like: What is happening? What should be happening instead? What are the boundaries of the problem?

Example: Students’ quiz answers are not being saved when submitted.

2. Gather Information

Collect all relevant data that can help understand the problem. This can include asking questions, observing the process, and reviewing documentation or logs.

Example: Check the website code, ask students and teachers when the issue occurs, and look at error logs.

3. Analyse the Information

Examine the gathered information to identify the root cause of the problem. Look for patterns and break the problem into smaller parts.

Example: The problem only happens on a specific browser due to incompatibility between the browser and database-saving code.

4. Make a Plan of Action

Decide the steps to solve the problem. Include what needs to be done, who will do it, required resources, and a timeline.

Example: Update the website code to fix browser compatibility, test it on all browsers, and deploy the fix.

5. Implement a Solution

Carry out the plan and monitor progress. Communicate with anyone affected and keep records of actions.

Example: Update the website code, test, deploy the fix, and inform teachers that the quiz feature is now working.

6. Review the Solution

Check whether the problem is solved and if there are any unintended consequences. Learn from the process for future problems.

Example: Students confirm quizzes are saving correctly, no new issues arise, and the fix is documented for future reference.

1.3.6 - Understand the interrelationships between problems and problem-solving strategies and make judgements about the suitability of strategies for solving the problems in digital software development

1. Understanding the Interrelationship

In digital software development, problems vary in type, complexity, and impact, and the strategies used to solve them should be chosen carefully. There is no one-size-fits-all solution.

Types of problems include:

  • Simple problems: Clear, small issues like a typo in code or a small bug in a function.
  • Complex problems: Multi-layered issues involving multiple components, such as integration bugs or performance bottlenecks.
  • Recurring problems: Problems that appear repeatedly, like user interface glitches under certain conditions.

The nature of the problem directly affects which problem-solving strategy is most suitable.

2. Common Problem-Solving Strategies in Software Development

  • Top-Down Approach: Break the system into smaller parts, solve the high-level design first, then the details. Best for large-scale problems where understanding the system structure is essential.
  • Bottom-Up Approach: Build reliable components first, then integrate them. Suitable for problems where existing modules or libraries can be reused efficiently.
  • Modularisation: Divide problems into independent modules with specific functions. Effective for complex systems where teamwork and parallel development are needed.
  • Debugging and Root Cause Analysis: Focus on tracing errors and fixing their source. Best for specific issues or bugs in existing software.

3. Making Judgements About Strategy Suitability

When choosing a problem-solving strategy, consider:

  • Complexity of the Problem: Large, multi-component problems → Top-down or Modularisation. Small, localized issues → Debugging or Bottom-up.
  • Resources and Time Available: Limited time → reuse existing modules (Bottom-up). Ample time and planning → Top-down.
  • Team and Skills: Large teams → Modularisation for parallel work. Smaller teams → Debugging or Bottom-up works better.
  • Nature of the Software: Web apps with evolving requirements → Bottom-up or Modularisation. Embedded systems or safety-critical software → Top-down with careful design.

Key Takeaways

  • There is a direct relationship between the type of problem and the strategy chosen.
  • Judgement is required to match problems with strategies based on complexity, resources, team, and software nature.
  • Using the wrong strategy can waste time or introduce errors, so understanding this relationship is critical in software development.
