Subtopic 6.3: Data Taxonomy

6.3.1 Understand the different categories of data, their purpose, and how they are classified as quantitative or qualitative:

    Quantitative data: Data that can be measured and expressed numerically. Used for statistical analysis, calculations, and objective comparisons.

    • Example: Age, height, weight, temperature, income.

    Qualitative data: Descriptive data that cannot be directly measured numerically. Used to capture characteristics, opinions, or experiences.

    • Example: Colours, emotions, interview transcripts, product reviews.

6.3.2 Understand the definition and purpose of structured data, and why quantitative data is considered structured:

    Structured data is organised in a predefined format, such as tables, spreadsheets, or databases, making it easy to store, search, filter, and analyse.

    Example: Customer records with fields for name, age, and phone number.
    • Benefit: Quantitative data fits naturally, enabling automated analysis and reporting.
    • Drawback: Rigid format may not accommodate descriptive or multimedia data.

6.3.3 Understand the definition and purpose of unstructured data, and why qualitative data is usually unstructured:

    Unstructured data does not follow a predefined format and often includes rich descriptive information, multimedia, or text, making it more flexible but harder to process automatically.

    Example: Emails, photos, videos, social media posts.
    • Benefit: Captures detailed context and human insights that structured data cannot.
    • Drawback: Requires transformation or processing tools to analyse effectively.

6.3.4 Understand the different representations of quantitative data including discrete, continuous, and categorical values:

    Discrete values: Numerical values that can only take specific numbers.

    • Example: Number of students in a class.

    Continuous values: Numerical values that can take any value within a range.

    • Example: Height in centimeters (e.g., 165.4 cm).

    Categorical values: Numerical values representing categories or groups rather than quantities.

    • Example: 1 = Male, 2 = Female; 0 = No, 1 = Yes.

6.3.5 Understand the properties of qualitative data, how it is stored, retrieved, and codified into structured formats:

    Qualitative data is usually treated as a single object and can be codified into structured data for analysis.

    • Benefit: Preserves rich descriptive information for deeper insight.
    • Benefit: Can be transformed into structured formats (e.g., coding interview responses).
    Example: Tagging images with keywords or categorising survey responses into themes.

6.3.6 Understand the interrelationships between data categories, structures, and transformations, and evaluate their suitability in digital software development:

    Quantitative data is typically structured, making it easy to process and analyse. Qualitative data is often unstructured but can be transformed into structured formats for software applications and analytics.

    • Quantitative → Structured → Automated analysis and reporting.
    • Qualitative → Unstructured → Provides rich insights but requires processing tools.
    • Transformation bridges unstructured and structured data, e.g., sentiment analysis, image recognition, or tagging.

    Judgement examples:

    • Banking app: Structured quantitative data for balances, transactions, and reporting is most suitable.
    • Social media platform: Unstructured qualitative data for posts, images, and comments must be transformed for moderation and analytics.
