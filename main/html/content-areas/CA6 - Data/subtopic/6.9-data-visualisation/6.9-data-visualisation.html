Subtopic 6.9: Data Visualisation

6.9.1 Know and understand data visualisation formats and when they are used

Graphs

    Definition: Graphs are visual representations of numerical data, showing relationships, trends, or patterns.

    Common types: Line graphs, bar graphs, scatter plots.

    When used:

    • Line graphs: show trends over time.
    • Bar graphs: compare categories.
    • Scatter plots: show correlations between two variables.

Charts

    Definition: Charts simplify complex data into digestible pieces.

    Common types: Pie charts, column charts, area charts.

    When used:

    • Pie charts: show proportions of a whole.
    • Column charts: compare values across categories.
    • Area charts: emphasize volume or quantity changes over time.

Tables

    Definition: Tables organise data into rows and columns for precise reading and reference.

    When used:

    • Present exact numbers.
    • Compare multiple variables side by side.
    • Best for detailed datasets where precision is important.

Reports

    Definition: Reports are structured documents presenting data and analysis, often combining text, tables, and visuals.

    When used:

    • Summarise findings for stakeholders.
    • Provide context and explanations alongside data.
    • Ideal for formal decision-making.

Dashboards

    Definition: Dashboards are interactive visual displays that provide real-time data insights in a concise, user-friendly format.

    When used:

    • Monitor key performance indicators (KPIs).
    • Track multiple data sources at once.
    • Useful for managers or teams needing quick updates.

Infographics

    Definition: Infographics combine data, images, and text to tell a story.

    When used:

    • Communicate complex information quickly.
    • Engage general audiences with visual storytelling.
    • Ideal for marketing, education, or awareness campaigns.

6.9.2 Know and understand the benefits and drawbacks of data visualisation formats based on:

Type of Data

  • Graphs/Charts: Good for numerical trends; not ideal for large textual data.
  • Tables: Precise, suitable for detailed numbers; less visually engaging.
  • Dashboards: Effective for real-time quantitative data; can be overwhelming if cluttered.
  • Infographics: Simplifies complex data; may sacrifice precision for clarity.

Intended Audience

  • Specialists: Prefer detailed tables, charts, and dashboards.
  • General public: Prefer infographics and simple charts for easy understanding.
  • Managers/Executives: Dashboards or summary reports with visuals for quick decision-making.

Brief

  • Formal reports: Use tables, charts, and graphs with detailed analysis.
  • Marketing/Presentations: Infographics, colorful charts, and dashboards to engage attention.
  • Internal tracking: Dashboards to monitor KPIs and progress effectively.
