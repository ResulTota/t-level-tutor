Subtopic 6.7: Data Dimensions and Maintenance

6.7.1 Know the definitions of the six Vs (dimensions) and understand their impact

Volume

    Definition: The amount of data generated and stored.

    Impact: Large volumes require scalable storage solutions and efficient processing tools. Example: Social media platforms generate terabytes of data daily.

Variety

    Definition: Different types of data, including structured, semi-structured, and unstructured.

    Impact: Diverse data types require specialized methods to gather and process. Example: Text, images, videos, sensor readings, and transaction logs.

Variability

    Definition: The inconsistency of data flow and meaning over time.

    Impact: Makes standardization difficult and requires adaptable storage and processing systems. Example: Seasonal trends in customer purchase patterns.

Velocity

    Definition: The speed at which data is generated and processed.

    Impact: High-velocity data demands real-time processing. Example: Stock market feeds or streaming sensor data.

Veracity

    Definition: The accuracy, trustworthiness, and quality of data.

    Impact: Low veracity data can lead to poor decisions; requires validation and verification. Example: User-entered survey data may contain errors.

Value

    Definition: The usefulness of data for decision-making and business outcomes.

    Impact: Helps prioritize storage and processing resources. Example: Customer purchase history analyzed for targeted marketing.

6.7.2 Definition of Big Data and its multiple dimensions

    Definition: Extremely large datasets too complex for traditional tools.

    Multiple dimensions: Includes volume, variety, velocity, variability, veracity, and value, affecting how data is collected, stored, maintained, and analyzed.

    Example: A global e-commerce company managing millions of transactions per day across multiple platforms.

6.7.3 Impact of each dimension on data gathering and maintenance

    Volume: Requires scalable collection tools and efficient storage.

    Variety: Needs integration of different data formats.

    Variability: Demands adaptable storage and processing.

    Velocity: Requires real-time pipelines and fast updates.

    Veracity: Continuous cleansing, validation, and verification.

    Value: Prioritizes storage and maintenance for high-value data.

6.7.4 Data quality assurance methods and their purpose

Validation

    Definition: Ensuring data meets defined rules or criteria.

    Purpose: Prevents incorrect data entry. Example: Ensuring a date field contains valid dates.

Verification

    Definition: Confirming data is correct and consistent with the source.

    Purpose: Detects errors or mismatches. Example: Cross-checking customer info with official documents.

Reliability

    Definition: Ensures data consistently represents reality.

    Purpose: Supports confidence in decisions. Example: Sensor readings reflect actual temperature.

Consistency

    Definition: Data is uniform across datasets or systems.

    Purpose: Prevents conflicts between copies. Example: Customer email stored the same way in all databases.

Integrity

    Definition: Data is complete, accurate, and protected from unauthorized changes.

    Purpose: Maintains trustworthiness. Example: Database constraints prevent duplicate IDs.

Redundancy

    Definition: Duplication of data for backup and reliability.

    Purpose: Protects against data loss. Example: Cloud backups and mirrored servers.

6.7.5 Factors affecting data maintenance

    Time: Data can become obsolete; frequent updates are necessary. Example: Financial reports must be current.

    Skills: Trained personnel are needed for maintenance and quality assurance. Example: Data engineers and analysts.

    Cost: Storage, backup, and cleaning incur expenses. Example: Cloud storage and real-time processing pipelines.

6.7.6 Interrelationships and judgements in digital software development

    Interrelationships: Volume requires automation; velocity demands real-time verification; variety impacts consistency and integrity; veracity drives validation and reliability; value prioritizes important datasets.

    Judgements: Decide which datasets are worth maintaining based on value and veracity, determine quality assurance levels, balance costs, time, and skills. Example: Only reliable and relevant customer data is maintained for a recommendation engine.
