Subtopic 5.4: Technical change management

Technical change management is the structured approach organisations use to plan, implement and monitor changes to systems, processes, people and strategy. Effective change management minimises disruption, mitigates risk and improves the chance that the change delivers intended benefits.

5.4.1 Understand the internal factors that trigger change in organisations:

Organisational restructuring

Restructuring changes how teams, reporting lines or departments are organised to better align with strategy or improve efficiency.

Example: Merging sales and marketing into a single commercial team to reduce duplication and improve customer handoff.

    Benefits:

    • Removes duplicated roles and streamlines decision-making.
    • Improves alignment of resources with strategic priorities.

    Drawbacks:

    • May cause uncertainty, lowered morale or loss of key staff during transition.
    • Short-term productivity dips while people adapt to new roles.

Expansion

Growth, whether geographic, product, or customer base often requires new processes, systems and skills.

Example: A software firm opening a development office in another country to tap new talent and local markets.

    Benefits:

    • Increases market reach and potential revenue streams.
    • Diversifies risk across regions or products.

    Drawbacks:

    • Requires capital, hiring, and cultural integration efforts.
    • Operational complexity rises coordination and communication become harder.

Downsizing

Reducing workforce or scale to cut costs or refocus the organisation.

Example: Closing underperforming stores or consolidating teams to reduce overhead.

    Benefits:

    • Reduces recurring costs and can improve short-term financial health.
    • Allows the organisation to focus on core strengths.

    Drawbacks:

    • Can damage morale, reputation, and institutional knowledge.
    • Risks under-resourcing critical functions.

New strategic objectives

Shifts in strategy drive change across the organisation. Below are three common strategic drivers.

Diversification

    Entering new product lines or industries to spread risk and capture growth.

    • Benefit: New revenue sources and reduced dependence on a single market.
    • Drawback: May overextend capabilities and dilute brand identity.
    Example: A mobile phone manufacturer launching smart home devices.

Rebranding

    Altering brand identity, messaging or visual design to reach new audiences or change perception.

    • Benefit: Can rejuvenate the brand and attract new markets.
    • Drawback: Risk of alienating existing customers or high marketing cost.
    Example: A bank modernising its logo and customer interface to attract younger customers.

Additional features or services

    Adding functionality or services to increase customer value or competitiveness.

    • Benefit: Increases customer retention and differentiation.
    • Drawback: Higher operating costs and technical complexity; possible low adoption.
    Example: A streaming platform introducing live sports or offline downloads.

5.4.2 Understand the external factors that trigger change in organisations:

Political

  • Change in government: New policies, tax changes or trade treaties may alter operating conditions.
  • Conflict: Sanctions, instability or disruption to supply chains.
  • Shift in government priorities: New funding or regulation focus (e.g., renewable energy incentives).
Example: A new administration raises digital service taxes, requiring price or accounting changes.

Benefits:

  • Opportunities can arise if the organisation aligns early with new priorities (e.g., green grants).

Drawbacks:

  • Sudden policy changes can increase costs or limit market access.

Economic

  • Provision of new services: Emerging demand can create new opportunities.
  • Recession: Lowers spending and pressures margins.
  • Inflation: Drives up input costs and wages.
  • Interest rates: Affect borrowing, investment and cash flow planning.
  • Consumer trends: Changing preferences (e.g., sustainability) influence product choices.
  • New competitors: Startups or incumbents changing the competitive landscape.
  • Entering new markets: Requires localisation, compliance and marketing adjustments.
Example: During a recession a SaaS company might cut prices, offer flexible billing, or focus on retention rather than growth.

Benefits:

  • Adapting quickly to economic signals can capture market share or preserve margins.

Drawbacks:

  • Poorly timed changes (e.g., rapid expansion in a downturn) can be costly and risky.

Social

  • Changes in demographics: Ageing populations or changing household structures alter demand.
  • Market/social trends: Ethical consumption, health consciousness, etc.
  • Adapting to remote working: Flexible work policies, digital collaboration tools and new management styles.
  • Cultural expectations: Local norms may affect product design or marketing.
Example: A company adopts hybrid working, invests in collaboration tools and updates HR policies to support remote staff.

Benefits:

  • Improved employee satisfaction and access to broader talent pools.

Drawbacks:

  • Requires investment in secure systems, training and new management practices.

Technological

  • Emergence of new technologies: Artificial intelligence, cloud-native systems, etc.
  • Retirement of obsolete technologies: Legacy platforms reaching end-of-life.
  • System failure: Hardware or software breakdowns forcing rapid remediation.
  • Zero-day vulnerabilities: Security flaws that require immediate patches or mitigations.
Example: A bank migrates from on-premise servers to a managed cloud to scale more safely and reduce maintenance overhead.

Benefits:

  • Modern tech can improve performance, security and enable new capabilities.

Drawbacks:

  • Large upfront costs, integration effort and migration risk; security issues can be urgent and damaging.

Legal

  • New legislation: New laws may require policy, process or product changes (e.g., data protection).
  • Changes to legislation: Amendments to existing rules that require compliance updates.
Example: Updating privacy notices, data processing and security controls to comply with GDPR-like regulations.

Benefits:

  • Compliance reduces legal risk and can build customer trust.

Drawbacks:

  • Compliance can be costly, requires ongoing monitoring and may restrict some business models.

Environmental

  • Sustainability issues: Pressure to reduce carbon footprint and resource usage.
  • Pandemics: Public health crises forcing rapid operational change (e.g., remote work, supply chain reconfiguration).
  • Natural disasters: Floods, storms, earthquakes that disrupt facilities and logistics.
Example: A manufacturer invests in renewable energy and supply chain resilience to meet sustainability targets and reduce disruption risk.

Benefits:

  • Long-term cost savings, regulatory alignment and positive brand impact.

Drawbacks:

  • Upfront capital investment and the need for new capabilities or suppliers.

Note: Many real-world changes are triggered by multiple factors simultaneously (for example: a recession + new legislation + a technology failure). Practical change management recognises these interactions and plans holistically to address people, process, technology and governance together.

5.4.3 Understand how organisations can respond to change:

New or Amended Policies

Policies guide how an organisation operates and must sometimes change due to legislation, regulations, or internal priorities.

Example: A retail company introducing a new environmental policy to reduce plastic packaging.
  • Benefits:
    • Ensures compliance with laws and regulations.
    • Improves organisational reputation and trust.
  • Drawbacks:
    • Can require costly implementation.
    • Resistance from employees if seen as restrictive.

New or Amended Business Processes

Processes may be redesigned to increase efficiency, meet new customer needs, or respond to external pressures.

    Change in Staffing Numbers: Adjustments in staff levels based on demand, cost, or automation.

    Example: An airline hiring more staff during peak season, or a factory reducing staff after automation.

    Change in Delivery Schedules: Delivery times are modified to match customer demand or supply chain challenges.

    Example: A supermarket introducing flexible delivery slots during holiday periods.

    Change in Opening Hours: Businesses may extend or reduce opening hours to suit customer demand or financial viability.

    Example: A gym moving to 24/7 opening hours to compete with rivals.

New or Amended Products or Services

Adapting what is offered helps organisations stay relevant and competitive.

    Completely New Products or Services:

    Example: A tech company introducing a smartwatch.

    Next Generation Products or Services:

    Example: A car manufacturer releasing a new electric model.

    Minor Updates to Existing Products or Services:

    Example: A smartphone company releasing a software update.

New or Improved Digital Systems

Technology improvements can streamline operations and improve customer experience.

    Back-End Systems: Internal systems such as logistics, HR, or data management are upgraded for efficiency.

    Example: A retailer adopting a new stock management system.

    Customer-Facing Systems: Systems that interact directly with customers are improved or introduced.

    Example: A bank introducing a mobile banking app with AI support.

Improved Training

Training supports staff to adapt to new systems, policies, or processes.

Example: A hospital providing training on a new patient records system.
  • Benefits:
    • Improves employee skills and confidence.
    • Increases productivity and quality of service.
  • Drawbacks:
    • Training can be costly and time-consuming.
    • Short-term disruption while staff are trained.

Restructuring

Restructuring changes the way an organisation is organised to increase efficiency or adapt to new conditions.

    Change in Management Structure: Levels of management may be reduced or expanded.

    Example: A company removing middle managers to speed up decision-making.

    Redrawing of Boundaries: Departments or teams may be merged, split, or relocated to optimise efficiency.

    Example: A multinational company moving its customer service department overseas.

  • Benefits:
    • Improves efficiency and reduces duplication of work.
    • Can realign resources with strategic goals.
  • Drawbacks:
    • May cause uncertainty and lowered staff morale.
    • Short-term productivity loss while adapting to changes.

5.4.4 Understand the processes, benefits and drawbacks of the change management process:

Identifying Type of Change

    New System: Introduction of a completely new system to replace or complement existing operations.

    Example: Implementing a new CRM platform for managing customer relationships.

    Amendment to Existing System: Modifying or upgrading an existing system to improve performance or add functionality.

    Example: Adding a reporting module to existing accounting software.

Role of Change Advisory Board (CAB)

    Prioritise Change Requests: Assess urgency and allocate resources to critical changes first.

    Review Change Requests: Evaluate feasibility, risks, and alignment with business objectives.

    Stages of Approval: Multi-level approval including initial review, risk assessment, and final sign-off.

    Monitor Change Process:

    • Collate and analyse change data.
    • Check change implementation.
    • Take action to accelerate change.

    Provide Feedback: Inform stakeholders about progress, issues, and outcomes.

Identifying the Changes to be Made

    Using SMARTER Objectives:

    • Specific: Clearly define the change.
    • Measurable: Quantify expected outcomes.
    • Achievable: Ensure resources allow successful implementation.
    • Realistic: Consider constraints and limitations.
    • Time-bound: Set deadlines for completion.
    • Evaluated: Regularly assess effectiveness.
    • Reviewed: Update objectives based on lessons learned.

Identifying Impact of Change

    Measure/Forecast Positive and Negative Impact:

    • Predict benefits such as efficiency gains or cost reduction.
    • Identify risks like downtime, errors, or user resistance.

    Analysis of Positive and Negative Impact:

    • Weigh benefits against potential drawbacks to make informed decisions.

Allocation of Resources

  • Budget: Ensure financial resources are available.
  • Time: Schedule sufficient time for planning, implementation, and testing.
  • Staffing: Assign qualified personnel to roles.
  • Hardware and Software: Provide necessary technological support.

Identifying and Communicating Potential Risks and Desired Impact(s) to Stakeholders

  • Gain Acceptance: Encourage buy-in from affected stakeholders.
  • Ensure Compliance: Follow legal, regulatory, and organizational policies.
  • Communication: Transparent updates help prevent misunderstandings and resistance.

Configuration of the New System or Process

  • Integration with Legacy Systems: Ensure compatibility with existing software/hardware.
  • Maintaining Service During Change: Minimize downtime to avoid business disruption.

Importance of Fully Testing New Systems

  • Reproducibility of Results: Ensure consistent and accurate output.
  • Test Environment: Use controlled hardware and software setups to simulate real conditions.

Method of Implementing Change

  • Parallel: Run new and old systems together to compare results.
  • Phased: Introduce the system in stages.
  • Direct: Replace the old system immediately.
  • Pilot: Test the system in a limited environment before full rollout.

Documenting the Change Process

  • Ensuring Requirement Traceability: Assign responsibility and accountability for each task.
  • Maintaining Up-to-Date Information: Keep records current throughout the process.
  • Recording of All Decisions: Document rationale behind each choice.
  • Retaining Change Documentation: Archive for future reference and audits.
  • User Training Manuals: Provide clear instructions to end-users.

Importance of Rollback Planning

  • Backup Methodology: Regularly save data to prevent loss.
  • Backup Location: Secure physical or cloud storage of backups.
  • Recover Plan: Establish procedures to restore systems quickly in case of failure.

Identify Training Needs

  • New Training Requirements: Educate staff on new systems or processes.
  • Refresher Courses: Update existing knowledge and skills periodically.

Identify Methods of Monitoring Progress

  • Post-Progress Review: Evaluate performance after change implementation.
  • Adjustments: Make necessary modifications to improve effectiveness.

Version Control Software

  • Track and manage changes to code, documentation, and system configurations.
  • Allows rollback to previous versions and ensures collaborative consistency.

5.4.5 Understand the factors that determine the feasibility of a digital project

Benefits and Drawbacks

Evaluating the benefits and drawbacks of a digital project helps determine whether it is worth pursuing.

Financial Savings

Digital projects can reduce costs by automating processes, reducing manual labor, or decreasing operational expenses.

Example: Moving from paper-based systems to digital systems can save money on printing and storage.

Cost of Implementing Change

Introducing a new system may require upfront investment, including software purchase, staff training, or hardware upgrades.

Example: Purchasing a new ERP system and training staff to use it effectively.

Impact on Processes

  • Productivity Gains: Streamlines tasks, reduces errors, and frees staff for higher-value work.
  • Improved Communication: Collaboration tools and shared digital platforms make communication faster and more effective.
  • Improved Security: Advanced digital systems protect sensitive data better than older manual processes.

Provision of New Products

Digital projects may enable a company to offer new products or services that were not possible before.

Example: Launching a mobile app to deliver services that were previously in-person only.

Impact on Company Reputation

Successful digital implementation can enhance a company's reputation as innovative and efficient.

Example: Introducing a secure, user-friendly online service that improves customer trust.

Risks

Every digital project carries certain risks that could hinder its success.

  • Resistance to Change from Workforce: Employees may be reluctant to adopt new systems, fearing job displacement or difficulty learning new tools.
  • Misuse of New Systems: Incorrect or careless use of systems can lead to errors or security breaches.
  • Inadequate Support for New System: Lack of technical or managerial support reduces effectiveness.
  • Inadequate Knowledge of New System: Poor training or documentation limits system use.
  • Disruption Caused by Implementation: Transition phase may temporarily reduce productivity or affect customer service.

Constraints

Constraints are the limitations that must be considered when planning a digital project.

  • Budget: Limited financial resources can restrict project scope or quality.
  • Time: Tight deadlines may require prioritizing features or simplifying implementation.
  • Human and Technological Resources: Lack of skilled personnel or suitable technology can delay progress or reduce effectiveness.
